Why now?

     Politics and the media have always maintained a symbiotic relationship dependent on each other's resources when necessary. It's obvious social media has been playing a larger role compared to prior election cycles with newly polished user-tested logos, sponsored debates, live public reactions, and flocks of 140-character statements. Rumors regarding content suppression after an ex-Facebook employee claims that the company knowingly hides and reveals headlines on its newsfeed service tailored specifically to a user's preferences, has left many wondering whether we should trust large "media" tech giants (Facebook, Google, Twitter, etc.) with our news. But even if companies are filtering our content, we're still collecting information and that's what's important, right? Not quite, recent findings from Dartmouth College suggest the transition from print to screen (phones, tablets, computers) has actually "shortened [users'] attention spans and left little time for contemplative thought." If users' are now less comprehensive and are more inclined to just read a headline, blurb, or tweet, what are users' grasping then when reading? Sentiment? Validity? Popularity?
     Twitter's 140-character "tweet-centric" environment gives candidates and their teams an effective channel to reach a growing base of followers at the click of a button (Donald Trump: 7.49 million, Hillary Clinton: 5.89 million, Bernie Sanders: 1.82 million [recorded April 07]). Candidates now tweet to state/clarify their views on topics being reported by the press (reagardless of validity) and even berade other public officials with insults (such as "Lyin' Ted", "Crooked Hillary", and "Little Marco"). Jeremy Sherman, an eptimologist recently interviewed by the Washington Post, was quoted: "He's able to do it with intransigent certainty, the content is secondary. He's a master at this stuff it's a permanent quality he sticks to you. And it's a tar baby it sticks to you the more you fight it." Another study from NYU titled "The Grammar of Politics" found that "conservative politicians more often use nouns in making their points because that part of speech can 'satisfy psychological needs for order, stability and predictability.'" With the rise of these new technologies and the recent presumption of the GOP nominee, Donald Trump, many wonder if there are correlations between public online opinion and the "purest" form of public expression of opinion, voting at the ballot box. The last time a candidate with no political experience was nominated was in 1952 when the Republican Party selected Former U.S. Army General Dwight D. Eisenhower. To put times in perspective, Eisenhower had to win World War II for the general public to consider an outsider for the candidacy. In 2016, Donald Trump won Twitter.

Project Mission

Fueled by the general public's acceptance and false sense of security of social media. The misconception that social platforms were made for users by users when in reality these tech companies, like any other business in America, serve the interests of their stakeholders.

Project Goal

Aim to successfully replicate real 2016 primary results with simulated primary elections based off a collection of Twitter data and sentiment analysis for each politically-relevant tweet in order to determine each user's voting preference.
